Ecological Succession

Primary Succession

Occurs on bare rock. No soil exists (e.g., after a volcano). Pioneer species like lichens break rock into soil over long periods.

Secondary Succession

Occurs after a disturbance where soil remains (e.g., forest fire, abandoned farm). It is faster because the foundation is already present.

Species Roles

Quick Reference Guide

Foundation SpeciesCreate and define the community (engineers).
Keystone SpeciesSmall numbers, but a massive impact on the ecosystem.
Invasive SpeciesNon-native species that cause environmental or economic harm.

Corals may look more like plants because of their hardened surfaces, but they are actually animals. They grow in large groups to form coral reefs on the seafloor. By modifying the environment, they make it suitable for other organisms to live. We call species that play a unique and essential role in creating and defining their community “foundation species”.

We call species that have a disproportionately large effect on community structure “keystone species”. Gray wolves are a great example. They created more resilient elk herds, aspen trees recovered, and they built dams that supported a host of species. Parrotfish are also keystone species; they feed on macroalgae that would otherwise suffocate corals.

Finally, “invasive” species are introduced to areas outside their native range and cause harm. Japanese knotweed crowds out native plants, and Zebra mussels damage waterways and pipes.
