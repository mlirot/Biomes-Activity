3. Succession

Primary

Occurs in areas with no soil (bare rock). Pioneer species like lichens must break down rock first.

Secondary

Occurs after a disturbance where soil remains. Much faster than primary succession.

4. Species Roles

Corals may look more like plants because of their hardened surfaces, but they are actually animals. They grow in large groups to form coral reefs on the seafloor. By modifying the environment, they make it suitable for other organisms to live. We call species that play a unique and essential role in creating and defining their community “foundation species”. Kelps are another type of foundation species, creating vast kelp forests for other species to live within.

There are varying sizes of populations of each species in a community. Sometimes a species found in small numbers can have a surprisingly big impact on its community. We call species that have a disproportionately large effect on community structure “keystone species”. Gray wolves are a great example. When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park in 1995, the changes were remarkable. They created more resilient elk herds, aspen trees recovered, beaver colonies increased from 1 to 9, and they built dams that supported a whole other host of species. The effects of the wolves are still being researched and monitored. Parrotfish are also keystone species. They feed on macroalgae, found on corals. Without parrotfish, the macroalgae would completely cover the corals, suffocating and killing them. This would be bad news for the 25% of the world’s fishes who depend on coral reef.

And finally we have “invasive” species. These are species that have been introduced to areas outside of their native range and can cause or have caused harm. For example, Japanese knotweed (Reynoutria japonica) is found in North America and Europe. It is considered by the World Conservation Union as one of the world's worst invasive species. It quickly grows to form dense patches that completely crowd out native plants. Zebra mussels are another invasive species. They are believed to have spread by the passage of boats and ships from Eastern Europe to other parts of the world. They are now found as invasive species in Western Europe and North America, where they displace native species and damage waterways and pipes.

Quick Reference Guide

  • Foundation: Species that physically build or modify the habitat (e.g., Corals).
  • Keystone: Small population, but huge impact on ecosystem balance (e.g., Wolves).
  • Invasive: Non-native species that cause harm to the local environment (e.g., Zebra Mussels).
