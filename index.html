Ecological Succession

Primary Succession

Occurs on bare rock. No soil exists (e.g., after a volcano). Pioneer species like lichens break rock into soil over long periods.

Secondary Succession

Occurs after a disturbance where soil remains (e.g., forest fire, abandoned farm). It is faster because the foundation is already present.

Species Roles

Terms at a Glance

Foundation: Species that create/engineer the environment (e.g., Corals).
Keystone: Low population but huge impact on the ecosystem (e.g., Wolves).
Invasive: Non-native species that cause environmental harm.

Corals may look more like plants because of their hardened surfaces, but they are actually animals. They grow in large groups to form coral reefs on the seafloor. By modifying the environment, they make it suitable for other organisms to live. We call species that play a unique and essential role in creating and defining their community “foundation species”.

There are varying sizes of populations of each species in a community. Sometimes a species found in small numbers can have a surprisingly big impact on its community. We call species that have a disproportionately large effect on community structure “keystone species”. Gray wolves are a great example. They created more resilient elk herds, aspen trees recovered, and they built dams that supported a host of species. Parrotfish are also keystone species; they feed on macroalgae that would otherwise suffocate corals.

And finally we have “invasive” species. Japanese knotweed is found in North America and Europe. It quickly grows to form dense patches that completely crowd out native plants. Zebra mussels are another invasive species that displace native species and damage waterways and pipes.
