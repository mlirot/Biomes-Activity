3. Succession

Primary Succession

Starts from bare rock (no soil). Happens after volcanoes or glaciers. Takes a long time to build soil.

Secondary Succession

Starts with existing soil. Happens after fires or farming. Much faster than primary.

4. Species Roles

Foundation

Creates and defines the community by modifying the environment (e.g., Corals, Kelp).

Keystone

Has a huge effect despite small numbers. Maintains balance (e.g., Wolves, Parrotfish).

Invasive

Non-native species that cause harm by outcompeting locals (e.g., Zebra Mussels, Knotweed).

Corals may look more like plants because of their hardened surfaces, but they are actually animals. They grow in large groups to form coral reefs on the seafloor. By modifying the environment, they make it suitable for other organisms to live. We call species that play a unique and essential role in creating and defining their community “foundation species”. Kelps are another type of foundation species, creating vast kelp forests for other species to live within.

There are varying sizes of populations of each species in a community. Sometimes a species found in small numbers can have a surprisingly big impact on its community. We call species that have a disproportionately large effect on community structure “keystone species”. Gray wolves are a great example. When wolves were reintroduced to YellowStone National Park in 1995, the changes were remarkable. They created more resilient elk herds, aspen trees recovered, beaver colonies increased from 1 to 9, and they built dams that supported a whole other host of species. Parrotfish are also keystone species. They feed on macroalgae, found on corals. Without parrotfish, the macroalgae would completely cover the corals, suffocating and killing them.

And finally we have “invasive” species. These are species that have been introduced to areas outside of their native range and can cause or have caused harm. For example, Japanese knotweed quickly grows to form dense patches that completely crowd out native plants. Zebra mussels are another invasive species. They displace native species and damage waterways and pipes.
